Death and Existential Virtual Environment

Using Artificial Intelligence as a Means to Contemplate Our Mortality Through Death Reflection

Abstract

Death reflection is the deliberate contemplation and contextualization of one’s mortality, and is often explored in traditional media and art forms, such as novels, theatre, and film; but how can new media be used to encourage death reflection? This paper proposes an installation that aims to foster death reflection and existential contemplation. The Death and Existential Virtual Environment (DEVE) will consist of a room with a virtual environment projected onto a 360 degree screen. When a participant enters the room, they will be invited to communicate with a projected virtual agent (a conversational artificial intelligence (AI) agent) about death and existence in a question and answer discussion format.

Keywords

Computational Arts, Conversational AI, Death Anxiety, Death Awareness, Death Reflection, Existentialism, Virtual Agent

1 Introduction

Why is it necessary to reflect on death? Death anxiety is the feeling of panic, fear, or worry caused by thoughts of death or of being detached from the world. Studies have shown a significant link between death anxiety and depression, as well as existential concerns and depressive disorders , and as people all across the world grapple with the COVID-19 pandemic, death anxiety is something many are struggling with. When examining the pandemic, much of the behavioural and emotional responses exhibited by people, such as suppressing thoughts of death by refraining from reading or viewing news on the pandemic, denying one’s vulnerability to the virus, or trying to prevent death from the virus through excessive cleaning, can be understood through terror management theory (TMT), which proposes that fear of death drives much of human behaviour. While some develop adaptive ways of coping with this fear, such as building meaningful relationships or leaving a positive legacy, others develop maladaptive coping behaviours , such as the outright denial of the virus which leads to reckless behaviour. However, while death anxiety and avoidance can severely affect psychological well-being, research has shown that death reflection can contribute to greater mental well-being and enjoyment of life .

Death anxiety operates at the “hot” or experiential system based on a heuristic processing of death, i.e. the immediate formation of decisions and conclusions without much thought process, prompting impulsive self-protective responses in the form of inflexible worldviews. Death reflection on the other hand operates at the “cool” or rational system, and involves engaging in analytic and deliberate contemplation of the meaning of mortality . In recent studies, researchers conclude that rather than respond with hostility and self-protection after contemplating death or encountering death-related stimuli, some people show attitudes and behaviours focused on helping others and the world . A study by Cozzolino et al. , for example, found that participants in the death reflection condition tended to increase naturally unselfish behaviors, and Frias et al. too found that participants in the death reflection condition in their study showed enhanced gratitude compared with those in the death anxiety condition. Similarly, the findings in a study by Blackie et al. suggest that existential contemplation has implications for well-being enhancement.

2 Related Work

Today, conversational AI is being used for mental health care, such as providing clinicians with feedback on their psychotherapy, assessing language patterns of successful crisis interventions in text-based mental health conversations, and helping to increase engagement from those who may consider traditional talk therapy as stigmatizing, for issues such as PTSD, clinical depression, and adolescent stress . For instance, Xiaoice, a Microsoft chatbot designed to provide general social interaction and emotional support, has reportedly been used by over 100 million people worldwide .

When utilizing conversational AI for human-computer interaction scenarios, it may not be enough to simply use text and/or voice input and output as an interface. In a study by Pontier & Siddiqui , a virtual agent guides users through the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) questionnaire, which is used to measure the severity of depression. The agent responds empathically to the answers given by the user by changing its facial expression, thus it resembles face to face therapy more than existing web-based self-help therapies. A response from several participants was that the virtual agent felt more personal and friendlier, and that it was more fun to perform the questionnaire with the virtual agent. In another virtual agent study by Montoro , researchers found that the use of the virtual agent can result in a higher degree of naturalness and “humanity” to the interaction with the system, with almost half of the participants (48%) preferring to interact with the virtual agent, while only 14% considered that they preferred interacting with the system without the virtual agent. Based on subjective experience, numerous reports indicate that interactions with animated agents are perceived by users to be engaging, entertaining, believable, comfortable, and usable .

However, caution must be taken when utilizing virtual agents. Cafaro et al. state that people cannot refrain from behaving nonverbally, and if they try to be passive, they may be perceived as unexpressive, inhibited, withdrawn, and uptight. Nonverbal behavior plays a fundamental yet subtle role in the dynamics of impression management, and virtual agents are not immune to these judgments. In their study, findings indicate that results in social psychology research on the assessment of personality traits and attitudes on the basis of nonverbal behavior between people also translate to user-agent interaction. Similarly, as Tsaia et al. touch upon, emotional contagion, which is the tendency to catch the emotions of other people, can also come about with the use of virtual agents, and applications should accurately account for it. In their study, they found that even a still image can have an emotional effect, and they state that as virtual agents extend beyond entertainment into emotionally-charged domains such as psychotherapy, the emotional impacts they might have on users must be taken into account.

3 Methodology

Through DEVE’s virtual agent, the participant will be given a sense of comfort, allowing them to feel as if they are communicating with another being. The virtual agent will have a mask-like face but will not have a body. The face will be human-like in appearance, but only enough to create a sense of familiarity while still remaining otherworldly, as if the participant is freely communicating to the universe itself. This will make it so that the participant feels as though they are communicating with something outside of their regular world, and perhaps allow them to feel reassured that anything that is communicated while in the space, will stay in the space. Through the use of appropriate gestures and facial expressions, the agent will be able to convey empathy as it accompanies the participant on a journey of death reflection.

As for DEVE’s projected environment, as de Melo & Paiva point out, digital technology is a ﬂexible medium for the expression of emotions, and in a virtual environment this can be achieved through the manipulation of certain elements such as light and colour. For instance, when mainly looking at Western cultures, well lit scenes can be regarded as happy and cheerful, while poorly lit scenes are mysterious and sad; red is associated with excitement or aggression, and blue with quiet or calmness . DEVE will present different emotions experienced by the participant as they communicate with the virtual agent, by mapping these emotions to the light, colour, and 3D textures of the projected environment.

Two techniques will be used to capture data for the triggering of the visual effects in the virtual environment. The first technique will use emotional analysis, a subcategory of natural language processing (NLP), which involves extracting specific emotions such as fear, joy, and sadness from speech and text through keyword recognition . In the case of DEVE, the emotions inferred from the participant’s speech, will be used to manipulate light and colour. The second technique will use the Performance and Art Cyber-Interoceptive System (PACIS), a wearable wireless device for analyzing real-time biophysical signals such as heart and brain signals . This will be used for the manipulation of the 3D textures, creating dynamic motion of the projected environment matching the energy of the participant’s biosignals. Having a visual representation of their emotions generated in real-time will allow participants to better connect with themselves and encourage an understanding of their own inner dialogue throughout the experience.

4 Conclusion

Death is something that all humans grapple with throughout their lives, and with the COVID-19 pandemic, death anxiety has taken a strong hold of the psychies of many. This proposed installation will examine three primary questions: a) how can AI agents be used to encourage human participants to reflect on uncomfortable subject matter such as death and their own mortality?; b) how effective is bio-sensory data in tracking emotional responses for affective computing?; and c) how does visual stimuli such as colour and light intensity representing different emotional states help encourage self reflection? A heightened awareness of death needn’t bring about fear and anxiety. Instead, through the use of new interactive technologies such as DEVE, this awareness can help to promote mental health and well-being.
